Neil Armstrong

Neil Armstrong was an American astronaut and aeronautical engineer who was the first person to walk on the Moon. He was also a naval aviator, test pilot, and university professor.



Early Life and Education

Neil Armstrong was born on August 5, 1930 in Wapakoneta, Ohio. He earned a Bachelor of Science degree in aeronautical engineering from Purdue University in 1955 and a Master of Science degree in aerospace engineering from the University of Southern California in 1970.

Career

Armstrong joined the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) in 1962 and was selected as an astronaut in 1963. In 1966, he commanded the Gemini 8 mission, which performed the first successful docking of two spacecraft in orbit. He also served as backup commander for the Apollo 8 and 11 missions.

Moon Landing

On July 20, 1969, Armstrong and fellow astronaut Edwin "Buzz" Aldrin landed the Apollo 11 lunar module on the Moon's surface, while Michael Collins orbited above in the command module. Armstrong was the first to step out of the lunar module and onto the Moon's surface, famously declaring, "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind."

Later Life and Legacy

After returning from the Moon, Armstrong continued to work for NASA, serving as Deputy Associate Administrator for Aeronautics and as a member of the National Commission on Space. He retired from NASA in 1971 and taught engineering at the University of Cincinnati until 1979. Armstrong passed away on August 25, 2012 at the age of 82.

Achievements

  • First person to walk on the Moon
  • Commanded the Gemini 8 mission
  • Received the Presidential Medal of Freedom and the Congressional Space Medal of Honor

Fun Facts About Space

  • The Moon is about 238,855 miles from Earth.
  • The temperature on the Moon can range from about -280°F to 260°F.
  • The first satellite to be launched into orbit was the Soviet Union's Sputnik 1 in 1957.
  • There are over 18,000 known asteroids in our solar system.
  • The International Space Station weighs over 900,000 pounds.
